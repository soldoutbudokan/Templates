# Niku Udon (Japanese Beef Noodle Soup)

Kenji-style sweet-savory simmered beef and onions over udon in dashi broth. Scaled for six, dried noodles.

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 40 minutes
**Servings:** 6

## Ingredients

### The Broth

- 10 cups water
- 2.5 tsp dashi powder such as Hondashi (or 3 dashi packets)
- 8 tbsp mirin
- 5 tbsp soy sauce

### The Beef

- 2 lbs thinly shaved beef — ribeye, brisket, or sukiyaki/cheesesteak cut
- 2 medium yellow onions, thinly sliced
- 2 bunches scallions
- 1 cup sake
- 4 tbsp mirin
- 4 tbsp soy sauce
- 4 tbsp granulated sugar

### To Serve

- 450g dried udon noodles (about 75g per serving)
- 6 to 12 eggs, soft- or hard-boiled, for topping (optional)
- 1 tsp shichimi togarashi (optional)

## Instructions

1. Slice the onions as thin as you can. Separate the scallions into whites and greens: cut the whites on a sharp angle for the pan, and slice the greens thin for garnish.

2. Bring the 10 cups water to a simmer in a large pot. Whisk in the dashi powder until dissolved, then stir in the broth mirin and soy sauce. Taste and add a pinch of salt if your dashi runs mild. Hold it on low.

3. In a Dutch oven or wide 12-inch skillet, combine the beef, sliced onions, and scallion whites. Add the sake, the beef mirin and soy sauce, the sugar, and 1 cup of the hot dashi from the pot. Bring to a simmer over medium-high, stirring to break the beef apart. Cook until the beef is done and the onions turn soft and translucent. Low and slow for 30 minutes only makes it better, and it reheats well.

4. Bring a second large pot of unsalted water to a rolling boil — at least 4 quarts, since 450g of dried udon needs room. Cook to the package time, usually 8 to 12 minutes, and start tasting a minute early. Drain, then rinse under cold running water and rub the strands with your hands until they stop feeling slick. Dip them back into the hot water for 10 seconds to warm through and drain again.

5. Ladle about 1.5 cups hot broth into each of six bowls. Lift the noodles with chopsticks, shake them so the strands line up, and lay them into the broth. Spoon the beef, onions, and some of the braising liquid over the top. Scatter the sliced scallion greens, halve a boiled egg over the top if using, and add the shichimi togarashi if you want the heat.

## Notes

Broth math: 10 cups total, 1 cup goes into the beef pan, leaving 9 for six bowls. Sugar is the dial to adjust — 4 tbsp lands on the sweeter Kenji side, so start with 3 if you want the soy forward. Cook only the noodles you plan to eat; dried udon keeps indefinitely in the box, cooked udon does not. Leftover beef keeps three days in the fridge and tastes better on day two.
